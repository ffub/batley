     George Bennet Jubb was born in 1859. He was the son of George Jubb and Rachel Field.
In the census of 7 April 1861 he was listed as the son of George Jubb at Lamplands, Batley.1
He was listed in the census of 2 April 1871 as a scholar boarding at a school at Clifton, York.2
In the census of 3 April 1881 he was listed as a manager and bookkeeper the son of George Jubb at Lamplands, Swiss House, Batley.3
George Bennet Jubb married Ada Sheard, daughter of Joseph Sheard and Sarah Ann Colbeck, on 16 August 1882 at St. Thomas's, Batley.


Jubb and Unna; a woollen mill in Fischamend, Austria

George Bennet Jubb must have moved to Fischamund in Austria soon after his marriage to superintend the firm of Jubb and Unna in which his elder brother Arthur Alma Jubb was a partner. From an account of Danube floods we learn that the mill had two waterwheels as well as an engine. By the 28th of December 1882 the waterwheels were out of action, described as "in backwater", and the engine stopped on the 30th, the mill being surrounded by water. George and Ada were living in Fischamend when a dreadful incident occurred in which George was injured.
On 2 January 1883 George Bennet Jubb was stabbed several times and and his cousin Joseph Marriott, was fatally wounded, dying early the following morning.

The Danube had burst its banks, and there was heavy flooding. It appears to have been on account of this that female weavers were housed in a barn in poor conditions. The story goes that George and his companions, Joe Marriott and Dick Carter, who had been in an adjacent brewhouse, went to see if they were warm enough, and was ordering wine to be warmed for the women when a young man, perhaps a sweetheart of one of the women, broke in. They tried to get him to leave, but he and his comrades attacked George and Joe.
In the census of 5 April 1891 he was listed as the head of household at Ebury Street, Batley, as a woollen manufacturer.4
In the census of 31 March 1901 he was listed as the head of household at 29 Carlinghow Lane, Batley, as living on his own means.5
George Bennet Jubb died on 23 May 1906.

He married Isabel Jackson, daughter of Richard Jackson, on 8 September 1818 at Northalllerton.2
In 1826 John Nussey was the builder of Carlinghow Mills. The mills burnt down in 1831 but were rebuilt.3
From 1835 John Nussey was the senior partner in the Smithies Mill at Birstall.4
In the census of 6 June 1841 he was listed as the head of household at Batley as a woollen manufacturer. Two female servants and two labourers were alos shown on the consus.5
In the census of 30 March 1851 he was listed as the head of household at White Lee, Batley, as a widower, a Woollen manufacturer and farmer of 60 acres employing 166 men 42 women and 123 boys and girls.6
He is said to have assisted in the setting up of the business in Berlin later owned by John Blackburn.7
In 1856 John Nussey was was the first president of the Batley Chamber of Commerce, founded in that year.8
He was listed in the census of 7 April 1861 at White Lee House, Batley, as a widower aged 74, a woollen manufacturer and merchant employing 329 hands, with a house servant and cook.9
It is said that John Middlebrook joined John Nussey's business at Carlinghow Mill to manage the financial side.10
In 1868 John Nussey lost money through the failure of the Leeds Banking Company and Carlinghow Mill passed into the hands of a company called John Nussey & Co, partners being John Barran, John Middlebrook , Henry Brooke and Edwin Wilford.
He was listed in the census of 2 April 1871 at Smithies Moor, Batley, as a widower aged 84, a woollen manufacturer and merchant.11
John Nussey died in 1879
; aged 92.12,3 He was buried on 10 September 1879 at Birstall.13
