     Charles Sheard was born in 1825. He was the son of George Sheard and Rebecca Blakeley. Charles Sheard was baptized on 8 January 1826 at Batley.
In the census of 6 June 1841 he was listed as a woollen weaver  with his grandmother Grace Sheard whose son, his father George, had made a second marriage to Martha Talbot the previous year.1
Charles Sheard married Elizabeth (?).
In 1856 Charles Sheard was listed as a rag merchant at at Pile St, Temple Gate, Bristol.2
In 1858 Charles Sheard was in financial difficulties, and January saw him imprisoned in Bristol Gaol for debt, described as a marine store dealer. Later reports of bankruptcy proceedings speak of him as dealing in rags and also as a Flock and Shoddy manufacturer.3
He was listed as the head of household in the 7 April 1861 census at 24 Castle Green, Bristol, as an agent in Rags(?).4
He married Hannah Preston, daughter of William Gomersall and Esther Bruce, on 8 June 1869 at the Parish Church, Batley, The marriage was by licence and witnesses were Joseph Chadwick and Elizabeth Crowther(?).5,6
In the census of 2 April 1871 he was listed as the head of household at Ward's Hill, Batley, as a Rag Dealer.7
In the census of 3 April 1881 he was listed as the head of household at Albert St, Batley, as a rag dealer.8
Charles Sheard died on 21 March 1887 at Batley.
He was buried on 23 March 1887 at The cemetery, Batley, ; described as a rag merchant of Albion Strret.9
An entry in deaths column of the "Roman Citizen" Rome, Oneida County, New York State for the 8th April 1887 says:-
"At Bateley(sic) Yorks, England March 21 1887 Charles Sheard, father of Mrs Thomas Scatcherd of Utica, New York and the Revd Charles Sheard of East Springfield." Brother of Titus Sheard of Little Falls, New York."

     Arthur Alma Jubb was born on 27 October 1854.1 He was the son of George Jubb and Rachel Field. Arthur Alma Jubb was baptized on 24 December 1854 at Batley.1
In the census of 7 April 1861 he was listed as a scholar the son of George Jubb at Lamplands, Batley.2

Arthur Alma Jubb was a partner in the firm of Jubb and Unna at Fischamend, a town on the Danube about 13 miles from Vienna. The firm is said to have employed about 100 hands, with some half dozen Batley men in charge of the various departments.3

He married Mary Ellen Critchley, daughter of James Critchley and Sarah Jane Illingworth, in 1880.4
In the census of 3 April 1881 he was listed as the head of household at Upper Batley as a woollen cloth manufacturer.5
Arthur Alma Jubb's brother George Bennet Jubb lived in Austria for a time to superintend the firm.
In the census of 5 April 1891 he was listed as the head of household at Southfield House, Upper Batley, Batley, as a woollen manufacturer aged 36.6
In the census of 31 March 1901 he was listed as the head of household at Upper Batley as a retired woollen manufacturer aged 46. Four domestic servants are kept.7
Arthur Alma Jubb died in 1904.

     Mary Sheard was born in 1830. She was the daughter of David Sheard and Martha Talbot. Mary Sheard was baptized on 28 February 1830 at Batley.
She appeared on the census of 6 June 1841 in the household of her step-father George Sheard at New Batley, Batley.1 Mary Sheard married Francis Senior, son of Squire Senior and Frances Hall, on 17 September 1848 at Batley.2
In the census of 30 March 1851 Mary Sheard was listed at New Road, Batley, as wife of Francis Senior.3
Mary Sheard received a legacy of £50 in will of uncle George Sheard in 1855.
Mary and Francis emigrated on 18 August 1856 sailing on the Ontario from Liverpool with their two children and Mary's half-brother; Matilda Senior, James S Senior and Titus Sheard .Titus went with them, apparently, as a last minute decision at his pleading. They landed at Castle Garden, New York on September 29th after a 6 week voyage.4
In the census of 7 July 1860 Mary Senior was listed at Little Falls, Herkimer, New York, as wife of Francis Senior.5
In the census of 29 June 1870 she was listed as the head of household at Little Falls, Herkimer, New York, as keeping house. Value of Real Estate owned is given as $6,000 and Personal Estate as $4,000.
Mary is living next to her sister Isabella and her husband Jeremiah Mitchell.6
In 1889 Mary Senior visited England with her sister in law and nieces Isabella Mitchell, Martha Clafton and Ann Sheard.
The party left New York in June and returned in October, arriving on the 15th on the "Aurania."7
She was listed in the census of 9 June 1900 at Little Falls, Herkimer, New York, as a knit goods manufacturer.8 Mary Sheard died in 1902.9

  • Matilda Senior b. 19 Nov 1853
  • James S Senior+ b. Mar 1856, d. 1913
  • Francis M Senior+ b. 24 May 1862, d. 4 Aug 1930

According to a family story, Jonas's defection was quite a setback for the firm
Learning his trade from his father, William, he was privy to the secrets of the business. At the age of 19 or 20 he was purported to have abandoned the business and have stolen/copied/taken the formulae to the USA and to Germany, which was at that time a major competitor to England in aniline dyestuffs. He was thought to have sold them or worked for a short time in Germany, and was lost track of by the family".7

It is hard to know how to square this with Jonas's recorded travels to America. It may be rather that he took the secrets there, but I think it quite possible that he went to Germany sometime in the early 1870s. It will be seen from my account of John Blackburn that there were Batley contacts in Berlin.
In the census of 3 April 1881 he was listed as the head of household at Grosvenor road, Batley, as a dyer, heavy woollens.8
In the census of 5 April 1891 he was listed as the head of household at Bath Street, Batley, as a dyer aged 63.9

Theodore Cooke Taylor told Douglas Sheard that he had, as a very young man, been in partnership with his great grandfather William. It would seem from the births there of his children that William had spent some time in Leeds, perhaps gaining experience in the dyeing trade. but he was back in Batley by 1870 and this could well have been at that time.7
William's career spanned the period which saw the development of synthetic dyes, the first, mauveine. which produced an intense purple, being discoverd by accisent in 1856 by a William Perkins who was trying to synthesise quinine as a cure for malaria. Based on a coal tar product called aniline, he saw its potential and patented it. However, other chemical companies experimented further, and developed a wide range of synthetic colours. Many were coal tar derivatives, so aniline became a generic name for synthetic dyes. A German company, BASF, beat Perkins to a patent for alizarin crimson, and Germany came to dominate dye production, but despite initial secrecy these dyes were soon available. My illustration from the 1870s shows local availability. William Sheard is said to have built up a very successful business, since dyeing became highly specialized and a mystery for the uninitiated, such as mill owners. Dyers bought and mixed the dyestuffs and chemicals, supplied their own labour, and provided a service dyeing in their customers’ premises with the mills’ own dyeing equipment, thus having no outlay for capital investment.

The family legend of William is that he was a dramatic and hard living character. He was a Freemason, Master of Trafalgar Lodge Batley No. 971 from 1863 until his death in 1891. He used to play cards and roust in the nights and, in full evening dress, ride a fine horse into the various dyehouses for the first shift of the morning, organizing the work from the saddle!7

     Reginald Sheard was born on 12 January 1878. He was the son of John Sheard and Margaret Maria Hodgson Hogg.
In the census of 3 April 1881 he was listed as the son of John Sheard at Bleak House, Upper Batley, Batley.1
In the census of 5 April 1891 he was listed as the step-son of Edwin Sheard at 165 Hyde Park Road, Headingley, Leeds.2
In the census of 31 March 1901 he was listed as an auctioneer's clerk the son of Margaret Maria Hodgson Hogg at 165 Hyde Park Road, Headingley, Leeds.3
Reginald Sheard married Florence Allen in 1902 at Leeds.4
In the census of 2 April 1911 he was listed as the head of household at 13 Bewdley Hill, Kidderminster, Worcestershire, as a tanner's managing clerk. There is a general servant Sarah Crew.5
Reginald Sheard died on 29 September 1914 of a cerebral haemorrhage at age 36.6,7

Children of Reginald Sheard and Florence Allen

  • Florence Margaret Sheard b. 1908
  • Rosemary Florence Sheard b. 1913
