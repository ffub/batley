William Colbeck

#1204, b. 1805, d. 2 July 1862

Photograph courtesy of Elizabeth Lancaster. ©
     William Colbeck was born in 1805 at Ireland. He was the son of James Colbeck.
William Colbeck married Hannah Armitage, daughter of William Armitage and Mary Farrar, on 8 June 1829 at Batley.1
In the census of 6 June 1841 he was listed as the head of household at Spa Hill, Batley, as a clothier.2
He was listed in the census of 30 March 1851 at Well Street, Cripplegate, London, as a merchant, lodging in the home of James Weyman, a porter, as is another merchant.3,4
William Colbeck died on 2 July 1862.5

He died in 1862, but Elizabeth Lancaster understands this photograph, with the name of a photographer in Philadelphia, to be of him, and that of his wife is certainly Hannah by comparison with a later one of her. William is known to have travelled on business, but we feel this is a rather early date for the photo. Any ideas? could it be a copy done in Philadelphia later of some images done by an early technique?

Relationship=4th great-uncle of Vivien Eley.
     James Thurman was born on 7 July 1803.1 He was baptized on 17 July 1803 at Batley.1
He was the son of James Thurman and Nancy Joy.
James Thurman married Charlotte Middlebrook, daughter of Joseph Middlebrook and Mary Ross, on 7 February 1830 at Batley witnesses Jeremiah Chadwick and Joshua Middlebrook.1
In the census of 6 June 1841 he was listed as the head of household at New Batley, Batley, as a woollen weaver.2
James Thurman married Rachel Stubley, daughter of Joshua Stubley and Mary Blackburn, in 1850.
In the census of 30 March 1851 he was listed as the head of household at Up Lane, Batley, as a woollen cloth weaver aged 47.3
He was listed as the head of household in the 7 April 1861 census at Up Lane, Batley, a woollen weaver, widower aged 57.4

James Thurman was for many years superintendant of Up Lane Sunday School.5
Isaac Binns tells of James Thurman keeping an old rope in his desk with which to chastise the disobedient ones. He tells us that the scholars at this school used to be taught arithmetic and writing, in addition to reading on the Sunday, and that this was all the education some people in Batley had.6
