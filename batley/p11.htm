     George Blakeley was born on 4 November 1806. He was the son of Joseph Blakeley and Mary Sheard. George Blakeley was baptized on 25 December 1806 at Batley.
He married Elizabeth Crawshaw, daughter of Philip Crawshaw and Susanna Senior, on 22 January 1829 at Dewsbury.
In the census of 6 June 1841 he was listed as the head of household at Batley Carr Road, Dewsbury, as a rag merchant.1
In the census of 30 March 1851 he was listed as the head of household at Hirst House, Batley Carr, Batley, as a manufacturer, firm of 2 employing 14 men and 44 women.2
In the census of 7 April 1861 he was listed as the head of household at Holling Royd House, Dewsbury, as a Merchant (woollen) Two female servants are kept.3
George Blakeley died on 2 April 1865 at age 58. J Willans mentions George as one of several Dewsbury gentlemen who had set up factories on the continent, and it may have been that these were set up at his instigation, but it was his son Benjamin who my researches revealed as living in Berlin for a time, whilst later another son, George Stanhope Blakeley, was involved with a business elsewhere in Germany.4,5
He made a will under 20K.
He had been in partnership with his sons Benjamin Blakeley and Edward Blakeley until his death.6

     John Herbert Blackburn was born on 22 June 1895. He was the son of John Sheard Blackburn and Emma Wilson. In the census of 31 March 1901 he was listed as the son of John Sheard Blackburn at Ivy Dene, Batley.1
John Herbert Blackburn was educated;  at Batley Grammar School and at Giggleswick.2 He was employed by in the family business at Batley Old Mill.

He began military service a few months after was broke out, in 1914 securing a commission in the K.O.Y.L.I, and for a long time did duty as a musketry officer. Early in August 1916 he went to France, and on the last day of the month he was wounded in the knee and shoulder. The wounds were not serious and after recovery he was engaged for a time on home duty. It was on January 5th 1917 that he returned to the Front. He was soon admitted to hospital suffering from bronchitis and pleurisy, and initial hopes of recovery were not to be fulfilled.2
He died on 8 February 1917 at France aged 21. He was buried at Lijssenththoek Military Cemetery, Poperinghe.

     Emma Beatrice Binns was born on 15 January 1885 at Bradford.1 She was the daughter of John Binns and Hannah Clayton. Emma Beatrice Binns married Harry Blackburn, son of John William Blackburn and Eliza Rebecca Sheard, on 7 November 1909 at Zion Chapel, Batley, An account of the marriage from a local paper describes Emma as niece of Mr Simon Cooper of Field Hill, Batley.
We are told that "The bride was attired in a charming costume of fawn faced cloth, handsomely trimmed with fancy black silk braid and bands of silk satin, finished off with hanging ornaments of jet. The suit was completed with a waistcoat of gold and white brocade silk. She also wore a lovely picture hat of black panne velvet draped with tulle and handsome feathers."1

After a time Emma Beatrice and Harry moved into "The Laurels" in Upper Batley, a house which apears to be an adaptation of the outbuildings of Samuel Senior's "The Rookery".1,2
In the census of 2 April 1911 Emma Beatrice Blackburn was listed at Mostyn Villa, Batley, as wife of Harry Blackburn.3
Emma Beatrice Blackburn was left a weekly allowance in her father-in-law John William Blackburn's will of on 3 July 1936.
Emma Beatrice Blackburn died on 2 July 1979 at Enfield, New South Wales, Australia, at age 94.1
