The Mill at Köpenick.

Although Willans attributed it to his father George, who may have been the instigator of the scheme, it is Benjamin Blakeley who was recorded as the acquirer of a plot of land in Köpenick, near Berlin, close to the junction of the rivers Spree and Dahme, and opposite the 1842 railway station. On the 10th December 1853 he submitted a planning application for a factory building, boilerhouse and steam engine building, and this was approved in May of 1854.3
In 1854 and 1855 Benjamin Blakeley was listed in a Berlin Directory as Kaufmann (merchant) at Mühlenstrasse 33, Berlin.4

Back in England on 18 September 1856 Benjamin married Maria Louisa Wooler, daughter of William Moore Wooler and Sidney Maria Allbutt, at Dewsbury.5

German records tell of a a dwellinghouse being built in that year by the mill in Köpenick and towards the end of the year Benjamin was living there with his bride.
In the early 1860s there were enlargements to the premises including sheds and stables. A visit from the factory inspector in 1872 seems to have required some modification of procedures. At this time two machines were mentioned, although it is not clear whether this refers to rag machines, and a figure of 125 employees is mentioned.3
In that year and until 1860 we find him listed in a Berlin Directory as Kaufmann and Fabriksitzer (factory owner).6
In 1857 Benjamin Blakeley was listed as Kauffmann und Schoddinwollenfabrikant, now at at Mühlerstrasse 59-60.4
In 1859 Benjamin Blakeley was listed in a Berlin Directory as a merchant and shoddy wool manufacturer (Kaufman und shoddywollen fabricant), and there are similar entries for several years in the 1860s.6
In 1861 a different address is given and this address is shown in 1862, 1864 and 1865 at Prenzlauerstrasse 47.4
In 1866 We learn from a notice in the London Gazette that Benjamin had been in partnership with his father George Blakeley , who had died the previous year, and brother Edward Blakeley.7 Benjamin pulled out of the partnership that year.7

His brothers Edward Blakeley and James Henry Blakeley were to carry on the mill at Köpenick for a time.7

In about 1875 the Köpenick Mill was sold to John Blackburn.
In the census of 3 April 1881 he was listed as the head of household at West Park Street, Dewsbury, as an oil and soap agent aged 51.8

     Max William Blakeley was born circa 1860 at Köpenick, Berlin. He was the son of Benjamin Blakeley and Maria Louisa Wooler.
In the census of 2 April 1871 he was listed as a scholar, his father seemingly away from home. the son of Maria Louisa Blakeley at Prospect House, Soothill.1
Max William Blakeley was articled to Michael Sheard and Walter Hanstock.2
In the census of 3 April 1881 he was listed as an architect's clerk aged 21 the son of Benjamin Blakeley at West Park Street, Dewsbury.3
When articled to the Batley practice Max William Blakeley made drawings of Howley Hall, Thornhill Hall, and also Oakwell Hall, where he is said to have spent a fortnight sketching , staying there with his second cousins, who at the time kept a school there.2
Sketches there included one of a fine ceiling in the drawing room, said to have been drawings given to Batley Museum.
It was as well that Max recorded this impressive ceiling for in 1882 it fell in on his cousin Susan Margaret Carter! A storm brought down a chimney, smashing through the roof and floor below, and Susan, who had been in bed in the chamber above, landed amongst its debris on top of the grand piano.2
 Max William Blakeley married Fanny Ethel Hewitt on 28 December 1898 at Holy Trinity Church, Chesterfielld.2
