Elizabeth Burnill

#3475, b. circa 1811, d. 12 June 1885
     Elizabeth Burnill was born circa 1811 at West Ardsley. She married Robert Brearley, son of Robert Brearley and Dorothy Hall, on 11 June 1832 at Rothwell.

In the census of 30 March 1851 Elizabeth Brearley was listed at Clerk Green, Batley, as wife of Robert Brearley.2

In the census of 2 April 1871 she was listed as the head of household at Dryfield Cottage, Healey, Batley, as a widow, deriving income from interest money.4
She was listed in the census of 3 April 1881 at Queen Street, Batley, aged 70, with one domestic servant, Elizabeth Goodall.5

William Hall

#3490, b. 1814, d. 23 November 1896

Descendants of Joseph Hall
     William Hall was born in 1814. He was the son of Joseph Hall and Elizabeth Taylor.
William Hall was baptized on 19 June 1814 at Batley Parish Church.1
He married Harriet Pycock on 27 May 1835 at Kingston upon Hull.2
Then a deputy constable at Batley, in 1842 William Hall followed a mob of plug rioters to Clerk Green Mill, and saw them tapping the boiler there following a leader's orders, then going on to other mills. Some of the manufacturers knew the leader's name as Dewhirst.3,4 The manufacturers affected were asking that the perpetrators were arrested, and William, summoned by the local magistrates, was asked to find Dewshirst. Magistrate John Beswicke Greenwood said that if William succeeded he would ensure that he got a reward.
William Hall was indeed able to arrest Dewhirst, recognising him the next day despite Dewhirst being disguised. With the assistance of a Frank Wormald, Esq, who was passing in his carriage, he was able to seize him, initially imprisoning him at Dewsbury. Dewhirst was regarded as so important a capture that a troop of soldiers attended William with him when he was taken to Thornhill Railway Station, en route to York for trial at the Assizes there.4
Having been able to arrest Dewhirst, William is understood to have received a Government reward of £50, fulfilling John Beswicke Greenwood's promise.4


Dewhirst was sentenced to a year in prison, served at York, but received something of a hero's welcome in Dewsbury on his release.

In 1844 William Hall was listed as parish constable.5
In the census of 30 March 1851 he was listed as the head of household at Clerk Green, Batley, assistant overseer at woollen mill (entered as Heall).6
On 12 May 1853 William Hall was appointed Superintendent Constable for the Dewsbury petty sessional division.7
On 20 September 1855 William Hall married Eliza Sharp, daughter of Samuel Sharp and Sarah Beaumont, at Batley.2,1
