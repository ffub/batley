Abraham Lister

#2070, b. 1828, d. 3 December 1878
Abraham Lister|b. 1828\nd. 3 Dec 1878|p69.htm#i2070|Joseph Lister|b. 7 May 1788|p39.htm#i1169|Jenny Raper||p69.htm#i2064|Jacob Lister|b. 1753\nd. 1802|p39.htm#i1162|Sarah Stubley|b. 1760|p34.htm#i1011|||||||

Relationship=2nd cousin 5 times removed of Vivien Eley.
image courtesy of Judith Lister
     Abraham Lister was born in 1828. He was the son of Joseph Lister and Jenny Raper. Abraham Lister was baptized on 17 February 1828 at Batley.1

In the census of 6 June 1841 he was listed as a woollen weaver. aged 13 the son of Joseph Lister at Havercroft, Batley.2
He was seemingly apprenticed to another trade, for In the census of 30 March 1851 he was listed as a master shoemaker aged 23 the son of Joseph Lister at Havercroft, Batley.3,4

Abraham Lister married Mary Ellen Barber on 4 November 1855 at Cathedral, Manchester, witnesses being Henry Brown and Mary Elliot. Abraham was described as the son of Joseph, a weaver, and Mary Ellen as the daughter of Joseph, a guard.5
In the census of 7 April 1861 he was listed as the head of household at Crosley St, Batley, as a shoe maker, master, aged 33.3,6
Abraham Lister was must, it seems to me, have been the Abraham in the advertisement shown. He is said to have given up shoemaking because, as he explained to his customers, he could not "stand sitting" though this grocery venture may not have lasted, for later he is listed as a bill-poster.
Abraham seems to have taken over the shop at the top of Billy Well Lane from Sarah Blakeley.
In the census of 2 April 1871 he was listed as the head of household at Spa Hill, Batley, as a bill poster.3,7

He was not merely a bill-poster, though; he wrote them, and his literary ambitions extended also to ventures in dialect almanacs popular at the time. These, modest booklets quite cheaply produced enabled the working man to play a part in recording the humour, attitudes and concerns of the time.
"T'Toadpipes Mill Almanac" was compiled by Abraham, and published in 1867 by Fearnsides of Batley.
Another which may only have had one issue was" T'Wooden Laddle Almenac", also published by Fearnsides.8
Abraham Lister was not the only Batley man to engage in writing these Almanacs, Isaac Binns was another.

In this website will be found mention of many involved in local politics, and who were elected to Batley Council. Whilst I think many of them made considerable efforts for their town, should they fall short Abraham was at hand to keep them up to the mark, and readily criticise what he saw as shortcomings. He is said never to have stood for election himself, but I daresay there were those who felt he should, and he certainly struck a pose of so doing in issueing posters with pithy comment at election times. My image shows the sort of poster to which I refer.3 John Lister has sent me copies of two more of these, which can be read by clicking on the text symbols here.9
I don't know how much education Abraham had himself, and if he sometimes slips into nonsense verse, this seems part humour, part consciousness of his limitations. Yet here was one who, with perhaps yet limited access to newspapers, was trying to keep abreast of national and world affairs and give voice to the concerns of the working man with a family to support.
Abraham Lister died on 3 December 1878.10 He was buried on 4 December 1878 at the Cemetery, Batley, That Abraham's opinions and criticisms were treated with some respect is indicated by the presence at his funeral of the then Mayor, Deputy Mayor and other councillors.3,11


Isaac Binns tells of Abraham Lister as a well known character:-

When asked on what date one of his children was born he answered "T'day after Bryan Hardwick's pig wor killed."- - - - - -

The first time I can recall Lister was when he lived in a little cottage which then existed in the Cross-street, which forms a continuation of Well Lane and joins Commercial and Wellington Streets - passing the Bull and Butcher Inn. Abe was at that time a cobbler, but he did not stick to his "last" -- I expect because he could not. He was a ready-witted being and never cut so deep with his wit as to cause offence. In fact his sayings caused the laugh to come from those who were the objects of his wit; and if ever a town could boast of a jester it was Batley when Abe lived. I believe Abe turned billposter from this house, and went around also with the bell as town crier. I hardly know a man more missed than Abe, for when no man steps into the place of a departed one, then surely that man may be said to be missed. Abe Lister was therefore missed, for he has had no successor in his inimitable line. At election times especially, Abe came to the front. True it was that at times his electioneering squips were of an unattractive class, but his yearly election speech was generally loked for and always out in good time. - - - - - - -Abe,- - - seemed to have business in his head when he issued his electioneering addresses; for, after telling all he would do and all he wouldn't do if elected to the Town Council, he used to put the stereotyped postscript---- "My price for posting is as follows:- Double demy, one sheet bills, per 100, so much, etc., etc." Abe was a free-lance in reality and hit all round. One of his squibs referrring to an election where Mr Eli Ibberson and Mr. Marriott were contesting for the municipal seat, I well recollect, and it is a fair sample of what he did at such times. It was thus----- "Vote for Eli and Tom; Eli likes water without any gin, but Tom likes it better with a little sup in." Abe also for several years came out as a publisher, his publication taking the shape of an annual called "t'Toad -pipes" Almanac. The almanac was written in the dialect, and was composed of original and selected anecdotes and witticisms dressed in dialect dress; the majority selected."12

     Mary Sheard was born on 14 September 1805 at Batley. She was the daughter of George Sheard and Elizabeth Firth. Mary Sheard was baptized on 13 October 1805 at Batley.1
She married John Blakeley, son of Samuel Blakeley and Elizabeth Stubley, on 23 February 1829 at Batley.1
In the census of 6 June 1841 Mary Blakeley was listed at New Batley, Batley, as wife of John Blakeley.2

Children of Mary Sheard and John Blakeley

  • Grace Blakeley b. c 1829
  • Charlotte Blakeley b. 1831
  • Jabez Blakeley b. c 1833
  • Elizabeth Blakeley b. c 1835
  • George Blakeley+ b. c 1836
  • Emma Blakeley b. c 1841
